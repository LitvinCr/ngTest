Sharing, Saving and Retrieving Work

It is vital that you have a system for saving and sharing student work in order to achieve the best learning possible, and to present evidence of learning to peers, parents and inspectors.

Students will need to be able to save their work at the end of every and retrieve it at the beginning of the next step if they are going to be able to create a project. Once the work is complete you may wish to share it with other people outside the class/ school. We’ll help you do that with some easy tips.

You can share and save student work through a variety of methods. Let’s look at the most common and accessible ones

  • Your server/ shared space
  • Dropbox
  • Google Drive

Saving and Retrieving Work

Tablets

Tablets save the vast majority of work as the students are working. Here’s some tips for helping them retrieve it. There are more complex methods, but we’re going to keep things simple.

  • Number your devices using a screensaver or a physical label so the students get the same one back every week
  • Students to save their work using their name (they can add something after if they so wish); e.g. Muhammed and Sarah; or Muhammed and Sarah - Ancient Egypt Documentary
  • When finished you will use Dropbox or Google Drive to get the work off the tablets and onto the school network/ website/ cloud space (to easily share with parents/ school community). See below for tips on Dropbox and Google Drive.

Desktops/ Laptops

If you have a shared space then you should have a class folder with individual folders for your students. Getting them familiar with accessing their personal folder should be focus from early on in primary school.

If there is no shared area then students will need to get the same computer every week to ensure progress with a project.

If you are looking to share the work to the school website or with parents you can use Dropbox or Google Drive. See below.


Chromebooks

If you are using Chromebooks then you have no doubt set each student up with a Google Apps/ G Suite account. See Google Drive tips below. If you have not set these accounts up then please visit the Other Services page to see how we can help.


Dropbox and Google Drive

These are cloud storage opportunities that can be used in a variety of formats; app/ website/ program. You can get different subscriptions ranging from free to a variety of relatively cheap packages. Cloud storage means you can save your work anywhere and access it on any other device without having to remember your USB key, have room on your USB key or not have lost your USB key! Not only that, but you can share your files with other people easily - including large files that can't be emailed.

For both Dropbox and Google Drive you will need to create an account and sign in to the app/ desktop version to complete the steps below.


Dropbox

The app is invaluable for teaching and learning as students can save their work at the end of a project and you will have it all in one folder in the cloud; you can leave it all in the cloud or download it to your computer/ network.

You can use the app in 2 easy steps:

1. Adding files/ creating a new folder
  • Create a class folder using the three circles at the top of the screen > press them and select Create Folder
  • There will be minor differences if you are using the web version, but the layout is essentially the same
  • Create a folder inside this folder for your students; it can be a good learning experience for the students (as well as reducing teacher workload) if the students make their own folders inside the class folder




  • To put completed work in the folder, simply open the folder you wish to add files to (press Files on the bottom menu if you can’t see your folder)
  • Press the + button and select 'Upload Photos'. This will bring you to the camera roll and you can select images and videos. See image below.
  • To upload other work such as eBooks you will use the Share button inside the app and select Share to Dropbox. This will open Dropbox, ensure the file name is correct and then navigate to your folder and choose Save.




2. Sharing the Work Outside your Classroom/ School

You can access your folder online at www.dropbox.com or you can download the app. Both look quite similar when open. You can also share these files with anyone in the world - including huge files like movies etc. How do you do it? Easily. Log on to the Dropbox website and follow the instructions below.



  • Press the Share button (right hand side)
  • A Sharing pop-up menu appears
  • Select the editing rights (if it’s going outside the school you likely won’t want people having editing rights)
  • Include the email addresses in the pop-up box or select ‘Create a Link’ and copy and paste that into a regular email
  • Recipients will receive the same folder that you shared and will be able to view or download the content

Google Drive

We find the Dropbox app a bit easier to use at the moment, no doubt Google will soon have rectified this. That said, Google Drive is excellent and if you are using Google Apps/ G Suite then you may decide that it’s easier to stick with Google Drive.

You can use the app in 2 easy steps:

1. Adding files/ creating a new folder

  • Create a class folder using the Plus button (in the app) or the New button (desktop) and select folder
  • The app and desktop versions have minor differences, but the layout is essentially the same




  • Create a folder inside this folder for your students; it can be a good learning experience for the students (as well as reducing teacher workload) if the students make their own folders inside the class folder
  • To put completed work in the folder, simply open the folder you wish to add files to. If you can’t locate your folder then; app - press the back arrow on the top left until you see it; desktop - press My Drive on the left hand side and use the menu
  • Press the + button and select 'Upload Photos'. This will bring you to the camera roll and you can select images and videos. See image below.
  • To upload other work such as eBooks you will use the Share button inside the app and select Share to Google Drive. This will open Google Drive, ensure the file name is correct and then navigate to your folder and choose Save.

2. Sharing the Work Outside your Classroom/ School

You can access your folder online at www.dropbox.com or you can download the app. Both look quite similar when open. You can also share these files with anyone in the world - including huge files like movies etc. How do you do it? Easily. Log on to the Dropbox website and follow the instructions below.



  • App - Press the three circles to the right of the folder (see above) and select Get Link
  • Desktop - right click on a folder and select Get Shareable Link
  • This will copy the link to the clipboard (you won’t see anything apart from a prompt telling you this has happened)
  • Go to your email and paste the link into the body of a new mail; tablet - hold screen and press paste; desktop - right click and select paste
  • To change the editing rights you can complete the above, except this time don’t choose Get Link, choose Add People (app) or Share (desktop) - you can actually input the email addresses here if that’s easier instead of copying and pasting the link as directed above.
    • Desktop - click advanced and change individuals or groups and select appropriate level of access; view only or can edit
    • App - click the three circles (top right of pop-up box) and choose ‘Who has access’. You can browse individuals or groups and select appropriate level of access; view only or can edit
  • Recipients will receive the same folder that you shared and will be able to view or download the content

Advanced:

Much like traditional hard drive storage you have folders in Google Drive and Dropbox which you can create and name appropriately. If you have Google Drive/ Dropbox installed on your PC or Mac it will appear as an actual folder in the menu beside 'My Documents' and 'Computer' etc. It's business as usual when saving to these folders locally on the computer. The advantage is that they will automatically begin to upload to your online account and become accessible anywhere in the world. This does require proper management of the space being used so it is not recommended to use this unless someone at your school has taken on the responsibility and done some trial work to familiarise themselves with its operation.


Sharing eBooks

You can share your eBooks with parents/ school community by using Dropbox or Google Drive. However, if the person receiving the email doesn’t have an iPad or suitable device for viewing the eBook then what do you do?

You can use a tablet or www.readium.org to read the eBooks (for Readium.org use Google Chrome as your browser and download the extension - you can then add any book and look at them on any computer including using the IWB).


Sharing Videos

You can share your videos with parents/ school community by using Dropbox or Google Drive. Is there a way of sharing it to your website or to parents in a way that it will play on all devices?

You can upload your video to YouTube (a​ nd change the setting so that it can’t be found by anyone except people who have been given the link), or Vimeo,​ or TeacherTube. W​ hatever method you choose, make sure they are set so the videos can’t be found by anyone by searching (the viewer should have to have the link to view it) - if the videos don’t have the students’ faces or personal/ school details then you likely won’t need to be so concerned with privacy (although it isn’t really any extra effort).

Once they are uploaded you should be able to add the video to your website easily. If your site is managed just send the link to the people who manage it. If you manage your own website then most web hosts have an easy to use Add Video function; you can generally just add this and then copy and paste the link onto it in your website editor - press Publish and your video is now on the website, ta da!
