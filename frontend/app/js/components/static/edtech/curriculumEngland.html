Curriculum expectations

We have divided the computing curriculum into three strands

  1. We have divided the curriculum into four strands;
    1. Computer Science (CS - programming/ code)
    2. Digital Literacy (DL - digital storytelling/ multimedia)
    3. IT (computer and online skills - including document creation)
  2. We have created a table that clearly illustrates the Department for Education curriculum expectations and their progression/ how they will be achieved from Year 1 to Year 6 (see page 4)

The curriculum should be embedded across all subject areas in order to maximise learning opportunities. In order to do so, we recommend reviewing yearly plans (for all subjects) at the beginning of the school year and identifying where technology best fits in. For example, if you know you are covering Ancient Egypt in summer term then look at the computing courses in your Educ8 Teacher Dashboard/ School Plan and think about which one lends itself to seamlessly integration with that area of study. Your Ancient Egypt project could be any of the three strands:

  1. Computer Science - students could program a game where a character moves around an Ancient Egypt themed landscape
  2. IT - students could research facts and create a presentation based on Ancient Egypt
  3. Digital Literacy - students could create a documentary or eBook on Ancient Egypt. They could even animate a specific process or general scene.

As teachers grow in confidence with the curriculum they will see more and more opportunities for seamless integration.


Computer Science

Also known as computer programming or code, computer science is the study of what it is that makes computers work. Students will learn what code is, what it’s used for and how to write their own code. This can be done in a variety of ways, including on tablets, on desktop computers/ laptops, online, with robotics and more.

Importantly for educators, students are learning a lot more than just how to program computers when they are learning to code. They are learning computational thinking and problem solving skills, evaluation and analysis skills, and of course there is a LOT of integration with maths. Look at our curriculum notes for specific curriculum goals as well as a glossary of terms


Information Technology (IT)

IT is the study of the use of technology, how to use it safely and effectively. It also involves creating data systems (collecting, gathering, analysing, presenting). Effective use of the internet includes understanding what the internet and the World Wide Web actually are. Students will develop their knowledge of computer networks and develop their online skills/ awareness. Online skills include effective search techniques, being discerning with digital content and search results, being safe online, cyber bullying and more. Other day to day uses of computers also come under the umbrella of IT - including word processing and typing skills, cloud computing, sharing and storage/ retrieval and more.


Digital Literacy

Using technology to create content is an incredibly powerful and flexible way to use computers for learning. Students can create interactive presentations, explore different digital storytelling media (film/ documentary, animation [stop motion and digital], eBooks), create and edit digital imagery and digital audio, and of course begin to combine the different technologies to make true multimedia projects.


Online Safety

We have included Online Safety on the table to illustrate where these important ICT goals fit in, though we aren’t counting Online Safety as a distinct strand. These criteria are should be constantly at the forefront when working with technology. They will be covered explicitly through the included Online Safety program of work but they should also be covered on an ongoing basis during teaching of IT and Digital Literacy. For the purpose of assessment we have included these criteria as part of the I.T. strand.

Achieving Curriculum Expectations

To achieve curriculum expectations we have created one scheme of work per term. Teachers should review their yearly plan for all subjects and assign the Computer Science, Digital Literacy and IT schemes of work to a given term based on best opportunities for integration. See the Six Year Plan document for more information. Online Safety will be taught every half term during the year; one lesson (or more) per half term

The schemes are six steps long; you may choose to do these in six lessons (this will be sufficient to achieve all curriculum expectations). Opportunities for extending the lessons exist within each step ensuring that you can spend more than one lesson on individual steps to develop the projects over the course of a full term if this is your preference. (dedicating a full term to each strand).

Please note that there are no curriculum expectations for EYFS, though we have provided schemes of work to aid their development and help your school achieve outstanding results throughout KS1 and KS2


Using Your Assessment Tool to Monitor Curriculum Progress

The assessment tool in the teacher dashboard has all curriculum targets separated conveniently into individual goals. The assessment tool has a spreadsheet style format; each curriculum goal has a designated column, and each student has their own row. Teachers will simply colour code the students accordingly with each curriculum goal.

Red - requires further support
Orange - achieving with support
Green - achieving expectations
Purple - exceeding expectations.

The sheet can be easily accessed by the Computing Coordinator or head teacher at any time. It can also be downloaded as a pdf and printed at any point for your convenience






Glossary

Algorithm - an algorithm is a list of instructions, like a food recipe. In programming, this simply means placing commands in order.

  • For example if you want a robot to drive in a square you could use the commands forward, left, forward, left, forward, left, forward.

Controlling or Simulating Physical systems - you can create a simulation world on-screen or control robots/ hardware to operate within your classroom.

Debug - an error in a program/ algorithm that prevents it doing what it should is called a bug. To debug is to remove the bug from the program.

  • For example, if your square algorithm above was ‘forward, right, forward, left, forward, left, forward’ you would find that your robot did not move in a square when you pressed start. Upon inspection you would see that the ‘right’ command should be a ‘left’; correcting this is called debugging.

Input - this is what you the user does when the program is running

  • For example, the user could use the arrow keys; they could tilt the iPad, they could press a button

Output - this is what you the computer does when the program is running

  • For example, when the user could use the arrow keys a character could move up, down, left or right; when the user tilts the iPad it could steer a car onscreen, when the user presses a button a song could play

Program - if an algorithm is a paragraph then the program is the full chapter. An algorithm could be the entire program, just like a paragraph could be an entire chapter.

  • For example, you may write an algorithm for a character in a game to move up, down, left and right when prompted. You could also write another algorithm for that character to lose a life if they bump into an obstacle. You may have numerous other algorithms all contributing toward that character’s behaviours. All these algorithms together are the character’s program

Repetition - this is used for more effective programming. It helps the programmer work quicker and neater and makes finding bugs easier.

  • For example, instead of doing the above algorithm for a square you could simply do ‘4 x forward, left’

Selection - a question is asked in the program and depending upon the answer the program takes one of two courses of action. This can also be called if, then

  • For example, if the character bumps into the fire then lose one life; if the character bumps into the pot of gold then score one point. This could also progress to if, then, else. If character has shield and bumps into the fire then lose no lives, else lose one life

Sequence - this is the order in which the commands are placed. It is possible to have the correct commands but to have them in the wrong sequence, so this is very important.

  • For example, in the algorithm example above, we see that ‘forward, left, forward, left, forward, left, forward’ makes a square. However if you change the sequence you will not get a square; forward, forward, left, left, forward, left, forward

Variable - this is a value that changes as the program plays out, it affects what happens when the program is running

  • For example, you could start a game with 3 lives. The game will end when this becomes 0 lives
